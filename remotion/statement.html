John Cooney's Run Elephant Run was the interactive story of an elephant's struggle to get home.

There are locations significant to the story, labelled for simplicity. To travel between these locations, the elephant must take certain modes of transportation travelling along certain routes; he must travel by limousine, by biplane, by dolphin, and so on. Taking the route allows for travel from location to location and vice versa.

The story was originally linear; that is, the locations had to be visited in order. So, John simply made routes, where the route simply connects the locations and .

Thinking about it more, however, John realized that he could have told a much more open-ended story. If he had maybe a different number of routes instead, maybe connecting different locations, the player would have had more choices on where to go. Realizing the untapped potential, he has thus decided that he wants to find as many ways as he can to make the routes; however, for simplicity, he wants all of the following to be true after making the new set of routes:

  • No route directly connects a location to itself;
  • No two routes directly connect the same pair of locations;
  • It is possible to travel from any location to any other location by taking a series of routes;
  • Starting from any location, if the elephant repeatedly takes routes, ensuring that he never takes the route he just took, he will eventually run out of routes to take, and thus end at some location. These locations where he can potentially end at for some starting location are called endings.
  • There are exactly endings.

We call a particular assignment of routes to locations a map. That is, a map is a set of unordered pairs , describing the locations the routes connect.

Now, John wants to know, what's the maximum number of maps he can create, such that every map has exactly locations, endings, and no two maps are similar?
