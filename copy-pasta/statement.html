Dr. Fagottini has a pasta collection, consisting of distinct pasta types. He also meticulously sorted his pasta alphabetically by name. He listed all of them in an inventory. And hence, he initially has an inventory with entries of different pasta types.

One day, as he was walking around his house, he slipped on a piece of cannelloni and accidentally tossed all pasta on the cloning machine he had in his house. As he was falling down, he accidentally pressed the CLONE button on the machine which made copies of the pasta that got tossed in the machine. Hence, after the cloning process, there were pieces of each pasta.

To make matters worse, after accidentally cloning his pasta, an earthquake occured and shuffled all the pasta inside the machine. As soon as he regained consciousness, he took the pasta one-by-one, listing them in his new inventory list. He ended up listing items in his inventory.

After writing the new inventory, he wanted to know how many copies of each pasta he has now (not counting the originals). He looked at the display of the cloning machine, which displayed . However, before he was able to see the number, lightning struck the machine, breaking the display.

Now, all he has is his inventory, consisting of (not necessarily distinct) items. Can you figure out the integer based on his new inventory?

Input Format

The first line of input contains , the number of test cases.

The first line of each test case contains the integer , as described in the problem statement.

The next lines show Dr. Fagottini's inventory. Each of these lines consists of a string consisting of lowercase letters from a to z. Each line represents an actual pasta piece that Dr. Fagottini took out from the cloning machine. You may assume that the strings do not have spaces.

Constraints

The pasta names consist of at most characters from a to z.

The input is always valid. This means that the items on the list was exactly what Dr. Fagottini retrieved from the cloning machine.

Explanation

In the first test case, there were two gnocchi and two balacchi. Hence, the cloning machine made one copy.

In the second test case, there were two gnocchi and two balacchi. Hence, the cloning machine made one copy.

In the third test case, all pasta Dr. Fagottini retrieved from the cloning machine were distinct. This means that the cloning machine made zero copies.

In the fourth test case, there were six hoy. We remind ourselves that Dr. Fagottini initially had distinct pasta types. So it could not have been possible for him to have started with more than one piece of hoy. Hence, we are sure that he started with exactly one hoy. Hence, we conclude that the cloning machine made five copies.
